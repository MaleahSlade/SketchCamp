Vincent Van Gogh (1853-1890)

When I took my son to see the Van Gogh Immersive Experience in Denver, I quickly came to learn that this was a complicated man, struggling to contain the war he fought within himself every day. Vincent van Gogh lived during a time when mental health was completely misunderstood and poorly treated. Using art as an outlet and coping mechanism, he implemented his masterful understanding of color theory to calm the storm within his mind. For example, it is believed that the reds and greens used within his painting, "The Night Café," were used to convey the tumultuous emotions he felt in that particular setting. "Vincent's Bedroom in Arles" is composed with warm and comforting colors, possibly to reflect van Gogh's need for a place of refuge and peace amidst his turbulent life. Unfortunately, the most heartbreaking part about van Gogh's story is that eventually art would not be able to save him from his disquieting thoughts. In despair of ever being able to overcome his loneliness or be cured, he brought his artistic career and life to an end in May of 1890, shooting himself and dying two days later.

Vincent van Gogh's story as an artist touched me in a way that I could not have expected. Like van Gogh, I use art to not only express myself but also to calm my mind and nerves. It quiets my most persistent ruminations, allowing me time to simply exist. But unlike van Gogh, I am extremely thankful that I have access to far more mental health resources. I wish van Gogh could have had that luxury as well.
